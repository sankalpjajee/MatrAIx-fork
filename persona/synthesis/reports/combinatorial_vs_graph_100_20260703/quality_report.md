# 🧪 Persona Sample Quality Report: Combinatorial vs Full DAG

Date: 2026-07-03

Samples reviewed:

- `combinatorial_persona8b_100.jsonl`: legacy combinatorial baseline sample generated before removing the old combinatorial generator folder
- `full_dag_graph_100.jsonl`: Full DAG graph-based sampler

Both files contain **100 personas × 1,290 attributes**. After syncing `persona/schema/dimensions.json` with the graph value space, both samples now have **0 schema mismatch**: no missing keys, no extra keys, and no non-canonical values.

## 🧭 Overall Quality Summary

**Winner for realistic persona generation: Full DAG graph sampler.**

The graph sample is much more believable as synthetic personas. It produces sparse, human-scale profiles: a few strong skills, a few real expertise areas, limited high-intensity tool usage, and fewer extreme language/culture claims. It still has some local consistency issues, but the overall shape feels closer to real people.

**Winner for strict rule-valid baseline: combinatorial + constraints sampler.**

The combinatorial sample is clean under its own hard constraints, but quality is weaker because most dimensions are still sampled independently. The result is often an implausible super-persona: dozens of expert domains, many master-level skills, many power-user tools, and many fluent/native languages at once.

### Final take

- ✅ **Use graph-based generation as the main quality direction.**
- ✅ **Use combinatorial+constraints as a baseline / stress test / constraint-debugging tool.**
- ⚠️ **Do not treat hard constraints alone as enough for realism.** They prevent some impossible combinations, but they do not create realistic marginal distributions or dependency structure.

A real person might be expert in 0-3 domains, advanced in a handful of skills, and fluent/native in 1-3 languages. The graph sample roughly moves in that direction. The combinatorial sample often gives one person dozens of expertises and fluent languages.

## 🧩 Combinatorial + Constraints Sample

### ✅ Strengths

- **Very clean formal validity.** The 100 generated personas pass `constraints.json`: 0 invalid.
- **Great schema coverage.** Every row has all 1,290 attributes.
- **Good stress-test baseline.** It explores many corners of the dimension space.
- **Easy to debug.** If a bad pair is encoded as a hard rule, the sampler can directly prevent it.

### ⚠️ Weaknesses

- **Too uniform / too independent.** Most dimensions are not meaningfully coupled.
- **Overloaded expertise.** Average persona has 56.33 high-level `fam_*` domains and 27.90 expert domains.
- **Overloaded skills/tools.** Average persona has 25.38 advanced/master skills and 27.08 regular/power-user tools.
- **Language inconsistency.** Many `Monolingual` personas still have lots of non-None `lang_*` proficiencies.
- **Hard rules are not enough.** They remove impossible pairs but do not create realistic population shape.

### 🔎 Quality impression

The combinatorial sample feels like a **valid attribute-space sampler**, not a realistic persona generator yet. It is useful for testing coverage, but many individual personas read as artificially over-capable.

Example pattern:

> A young or low-education persona can still have dozens of expert domains, many master skills, many power-user tools, and many fluent/native languages.

That is not a schema bug; it is a distribution/modeling issue.

## 🌐 Full DAG Graph Sample

### ✅ Strengths

- **Much better realism shape.** Expertise, skills, tools, hobbies, and passions are sparse.
- **Expertise is believable.** Average expert-domain count is 0.58, max 4.
- **Skills are believable.** Average advanced/master skill count is 3.64, max 9.
- **Tools are believable.** Average regular/power-user tool count is 3.36, max 8.
- **Languages are much better.** Average fluent/native `lang_*` count is 1.35, max 4.
- **Feels dependency-driven.** The sample looks shaped by a model rather than pure independent rolls.

### ⚠️ Weaknesses

- **Some local coherence gaps remain.** Examples: socioeconomic band vs household income, primary language vs English proficiency, seniority vs years experience.
- **Domain/industry coupling can be weak.** A primary domain can still appear with little/no matching industry exposure.
- **Language-grid coupling still needs work.** `Monolingual` should more tightly constrain `lang_*` values.
- **Some life-history combinations need stronger masks.** Relationship history, parenting, military/veteran status, health/disability, and developer/AI-tool usage could use more consistency checks.

### 🔎 Quality impression

The graph sample feels much closer to a real synthetic population. It is not perfect, but the errors are **targeted consistency issues**, not a fundamental density problem.

The graph generator already has the most important property for quality:

> Most attributes are low/neutral/none, while only a small number are high-intensity.

That is exactly what the combinatorial sample lacks.

## 🧠 Total Quality Conclusion

### Graph-based sample

✅ Better for realistic synthetic persona generation.

It has believable sparsity, better population-like shape, and much more plausible expertise/skill/tool/language distributions. The remaining problems are mostly local consistency gaps that can be improved with additional masks/CPTs.

### Combinatorial + constraints sample

✅ Better for controlled validity and stress testing.

It is formally clean and easy to debug, but it does not yet produce high-quality personas because the unconstrained dimensions behave too independently and too uniformly.

### Final recommendation

Use the **Full DAG graph sampler** as the primary generator. Keep the **combinatorial sampler** as a baseline, validator, and ablation tool.

The next quality-improvement step should be:

1. Add stronger graph masks for local consistency issues.
2. Add language-grid coupling: `multilingualism` + `primary_language` + `english_proficiency` + `lifex_languages_lifetime` + `lang_*`.
3. Add sparsity priors to combinatorial sampling if it is intended to produce realistic personas, not just coverage.
4. Re-run the 100-vs-100 comparison after those changes and update this report.
